The Human Layer · The swarm and the bee

One team, not stars

Security is a relay, not a solo sport. The brilliant lone engineer who knows how everything is wired isn't your strongest asset — he's your most expensive single point of failure.

Marc was the guy. Every MSP has one. He'd been there since the early days, he'd built half the client environments with his own hands, and he carried the whole map in his head — which tenant had the weird legacy firewall rule, why that one Conditional Access exclusion existed, the undocumented reason the backup job ran at 2:40 instead of 2:00. You didn't open a ticket on the tricky clients. You asked Marc.

Then Marc took two weeks in Portugal. And the team discovered, in real time, exactly how much of the company lived inside one man's skull. A client's email auth broke on day three. Nobody knew why the SPF record was structured the way it was, or whether the thing that looked wrong was actually load-bearing. The fix that would have taken Marc ten minutes took the rest of the team two days, a nervous client call, and a near-miss with a mail flow they didn't fully understand.

Marc came home tanned and rested to a team that had spent a fortnight afraid of its own infrastructure. That's the bus-factor-of-one moment — and it had nothing to do with Marc being bad at his job. He was great at it. That was the problem.

The rock star is a brittle design

The lone-hero model feels efficient. One person who knows everything resolves tickets fast, impresses clients, and saves you the messy overhead of writing things down. Right up until that person is on a plane, out sick, or out the door — at which point you learn that "efficient" and "resilient" were never the same word. Knowledge silos, single points of failure, a culture where the fastest path to looking indispensable is to be the only one who knows the thing: the rock-star shop optimizes for exactly the fragility that takes it down.

Marcus Aurelius, who spent his life thinking about how individuals fit into something larger, put it in three short clauses:

"That which is not good for the swarm, neither is it good for the bee." Marcus Aurelius · Meditations, Book 6 (George Long translation)

The Stoics had a word for this — sympatheia, the idea that the parts of a whole rise and fall together. The bee that hoards against the hive ends up in a weaker hive, which means a weaker bee. Applied to a service team it's almost literal: the engineer who wins by being the only one who knows is building a shop that's worse for everyone, himself included — because a shop that depends on one person is a shop that can't grow, can't take a holiday, and can't survive his eventual departure. His win and the team's win were never actually in tension. They only looked that way.

Covey called this the difference between a scarcity mentality and an abundance mentality — the quiet belief that there's only so much credit to go around, so a teammate's success comes out of your slice. His Habit 4, think win-win, is the antidote: in a healthy team, helping someone else succeed grows the whole pie. And Habit 6, synergy, is the payoff — a group that genuinely shares produces solutions none of its members could have reached alone. The whole becomes greater than the sum of the parts, but only if the parts are actually connected.

The professions worked this out a long time ago. David Maister, who spent a career studying what makes service firms great, drew a sharp line between two cultures. The warlord firm is a collection of competing rainmakers, each guarding his own clients and his own knowledge, each a star in his own kingdom. The one-firm firm rewards the group's success over individual heroics, grows its own talent instead of just poaching it, and treats knowledge-sharing as the job rather than a favour. Maister's finding was unambiguous: over the long run, the one-firm firm wins — it's more durable, more profitable, and a far better place to work.

Leading it

If you run the shop

You get the team your incentives build

If you celebrate the hero who parachutes in and saves the day, you'll grow heroes — and heroes hoard, because being the only one who knows is what makes them heroes. If you want a swarm instead of a collection of stars, you have to reward the swarm. People do what gets noticed.

  • Reward collaboration, not heroics. Maister's one-firm firm beats the warlord model precisely because it prizes group success over the star system. The engineer who quietly made three others better should be more celebrated than the one who solved it alone at midnight. Praise the relay, not just the sprint.
  • Kill the bus-factor-of-one on purpose. Single points of failure don't fix themselves; left alone they deepen. Name your Marc, then give every critical system a documented runbook and a second owner who's actually touched it. Pairing on the hard tickets isn't slower — it's how the knowledge stops living in one head.
  • Make sharing part of the job, not a favour. If documenting and teaching are unpaid overhead that competes with billable work, nobody does them. Build them in — written-down knowledge, deliberate hand-offs, time to teach the junior — and judge people partly on whether the team got stronger around them.
  • Build an abundance culture. Covey's point lands hardest at the top: if the shop runs on scarcity, helping a teammate feels like a threat to your standing. A leader who makes it safe — and rewarded — to share is building the only kind of team that compounds.
Living it

If you're on the team

The hero who shares becomes the senior, not the bottleneck

It's tempting to believe job security comes from being irreplaceable — from being the only one who knows. It's exactly backwards. The person who can't be promoted is the one who can't be replaced, because the shop can't afford to move him. The trusted senior is the one whose knowledge is everywhere, because he gave it away.

  • Write down what only you know. The undocumented exclusion, the reason behind the weird config, the order you run the steps — if it lives only in your head, it's a liability with your name on it. Write it down and you don't become less valuable. You become the person who made the whole team safer, which is what senior actually means.
  • Help the new tech without keeping score. Marcus's hive again: what's good for the swarm is good for the bee. Ten minutes spent unblocking a junior is not ten minutes stolen from your "real" work — it's how the team you depend on gets stronger. The score takes care of itself.
  • Bring problems to the team early. The instinct to disappear and emerge with the heroic solution feels impressive. Covey's synergy says the opposite pays better: surface the hard problem early and the group will routinely beat your single best idea. Asking for help is not weakness — it's how good answers get found.
  • Remember your teammate's win is not your loss. When the new hire nails a tricky migration, that's not a smaller slice for you — it's a stronger team carrying you on the next bad night. The shop that wins together is the one you actually want to be standing in when something breaks.

Portugal, replayed

Now run Marc's two weeks again, in a team that shared. The email auth breaks on day three exactly like before. But this time the SPF record's quirks are documented, the reason for the structure is written down in plain language, and three other people have actually worked that client's mail flow. The fix takes ten minutes. Nobody panics. The client never even knows there was a wobble.

Marc still comes home tanned and rested — to a team that genuinely missed him, not a smoking crater that survived him. He's no less valued; he's more, because the thing he built didn't fall over the moment he stepped away. That's what it looks like when the knowledge lives in the swarm instead of the bee.

The lone hero feels like strength right up until the day he's unavailable, and then you find out it was the most expensive fragility in the building. A team that shares is slower to look impressive and far harder to break. That trade — a little less individual glory for a lot more collective resilience — is the human layer, and it's the difference between a shop that depends on one person and a shop that can actually grow.

Try this this week

On the team: document one thing that currently lives only in your head — the why, not just the what. If you lead: name your bus-factor-of-one out loud, and give that knowledge a real second owner.
