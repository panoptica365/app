The Human Layer · Urgent vs. important

First things first

Security has an infinite supply of urgent. The work that actually keeps a tenant safe is quiet, unglamorous, and never on fire — which is exactly why it's the first thing to get skipped.

It's 8:05 a.m. You sit down with a plan: today you finally finish hardening the new client's Conditional Access — the work that's been sliding for three weeks. You open the policy. You read one line.

Then a printer at the dental office stops talking to the network. Then a password reset that "can't wait." Then a partner pings you with a "quick question" that takes forty minutes and a screen-share. By 11 you're elbow-deep in someone else's DNS. By 3 you're firefighting a mailbox rule that's forwarding invoices to a Gmail address. At 6 p.m. you close the laptop, genuinely spent — and the hardening project is exactly where you left it. One line in.

Nobody dropped the ball. Every single thing you touched was real. And yet the one piece of work that would actually have reduced risk this week didn't move an inch. That's not a discipline problem. It's a sorting problem — and it has a name.

The tyranny of the urgent

The trap is that urgent and important feel like the same thing, and they're not even close. Urgent things shout — a phone ringing, a red alert, a client who treats every ticket like a structure fire. Important things whisper — the backup test, the runbook nobody's updated, the training that would stop next month's incident. Urgent demands attention. Important deserves it. The two almost never line up, and when we're tired we let the shouting win.

Two thousand years ago, the man who ran the Roman Empire wrote himself a reminder about exactly this — how to handle a day with too much coming at it:

"Every moment think steadily as a Roman and a man to do what thou hast in hand with perfect and simple dignity." Marcus Aurelius · Meditations, Book 2 (George Long translation)

The Stoic move here is not to do more. It's to do one thing — the thing in hand — and to give it your whole, undivided self. Marcus's discipline was to confine himself to the present task instead of drowning in the whole queue at once. You cannot fight every fire in the same instant, so you stop trying to hold them all in your head and you pour yourself into the one in front of you. That sounds soft. On a chaotic morning it's the hardest skill there is.

Stephen Covey gave the modern version of this its sharpest tool: the time matrix. Sort everything you do along two axes — urgent or not, important or not — and you get four quadrants. Quadrant I is the important-and-urgent crisis you can't ignore. Quadrant III is the urgent-but-unimportant noise that masquerades as work. Quadrant IV is the time-wasting drift. And Quadrant II — important but not urgent — is where safety actually lives: the hardening, the documentation, the backup test, the training. Covey's whole point in Habit 3 was that Quadrant II is the only quadrant that prevents the others, and it's the first thing the urgent crowds out.

And here's the part Covey wrote and Paul Jarvis would underline twice: not every fire is yours, and not every urgent thing is even important. Jarvis built a whole philosophy of business around the word enough — doing less, but better; staying small enough to do the work that matters instead of just the work that's loud. The reflex to grab every ringing phone feels like service. Often it's just the urgent train running you instead of you running it.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Quadrant II won't defend itself — you have to

Important-but-not-urgent work has no advocate. It never escalates, never pages you, never shows up red on a dashboard. Left to the natural pull of the day, it loses every time to whatever's shouting. The only force that protects it is a leader who decides it's protected. That's a deliberate act, and it's yours alone to make.

  • Defend the block like an SLA. If a client SLA is sacred, a team's Quadrant II hour can be too. Put hardening, backup tests, and documentation on the calendar as real, shielded work — then guard it from drop-in fires the way you'd guard a maintenance window. Covey's whole argument is that you don't find time for the important; you schedule it first and fit the rest around it.
  • Decide which fires are actually yours. Jarvis's instinct for enough is a leadership tool: not every urgent thing that lands in the queue is important, and not every important thing is yours to fight. Triage ruthlessly. A shop that fights every fire equally will lose the one that mattered.
  • Measure prevention, not just velocity. Tickets closed is the easiest metric and the most misleading — it rewards Quadrant I and III and is blind to Quadrant II entirely. The patch that prevented an incident closes no ticket. If the only thing you count is reactive volume, you'll quietly punish the people doing the work that keeps you safe.
Living it

If you're on the team

Name the important thing before the inbox names it for you

You don't have to run the shop to run your own morning. The day will absolutely fill itself with urgent if you let it — so the move is to decide, before you open email, what the one thing that matters actually is. After that, you're defending a choice instead of reacting to a queue.

  • Pick the one thing first. Before the inbox, before the chat, before the first ring — name the single important-not-urgent task you want to have moved by end of day. Write it down. Now the day has a spine, and everything else has to negotiate with it.
  • Batch the reactive stuff. Context-switching is a tax, and it compounds. Marcus's discipline — one task, full attention — beats ten half-tasks every time. Where you can, gather the small interruptions into a window instead of letting each one detonate your focus the instant it arrives.
  • Learn to hold a "quick question." A quick question is rarely quick, and it's almost never as urgent as the fact that it interrupted you makes it feel. "Let me finish this and come back to you at 11" is a complete sentence. Most quick questions survive the wait. The ones that don't were real Quadrant I and you'll know.
  • Protect a daily block to get better. The reading, the lab, the cert chapter — that's Quadrant II for you, and it's the first thing the day eats. Defend a small block of it. The technician who never improves is always the busiest one in the room.

8:05 a.m., replayed

Now picture the same chaotic day, one decision earlier. You sit down at 8:05 — but you don't open email. You open the Conditional Access policy and you give it ninety minutes of full, undivided attention while the rest of the building is still quiet. The fires haven't woken up yet.

By 9:35 the printer is down, the password reset lands, the partner has a question. You handle all of it, same as before — the urgent didn't go anywhere, and almost none of it actually expired while it waited. But the hardening project isn't one line in anymore. It's done. Same chaos. Same fires. Same you. Different order.

That's the whole trick, and it's smaller than it sounds. The urgent will always be there to swallow your day if you let it go first. So you don't let it go first. You give the important thing the front of the morning — its perfect and simple dignity — and you let the noise fit around it. That choice, made before the inbox makes it for you, is the human layer.

Try this tomorrow

Do the one important-not-urgent thing first, before you open email. Then notice, honestly, how much of the urgent actually survived the wait.
