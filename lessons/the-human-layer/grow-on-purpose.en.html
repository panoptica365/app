The Human Layer · Deliberate growth

Grow on purpose

In a field that changes monthly, standing still isn't holding steady — it's sliding backward at the speed of everyone else moving forward.

Two techs were hired the same spring, off near-identical résumés. Three years on, one is the person clients ask for by name — she got the security cert, she's running incident triage, she's the one juniors quietly DM when they're stuck. The other is doing roughly the same tier-1 tickets he was doing in year one, a little more tired, a little more convinced the good breaks went to other people.

The difference wasn't talent. They started level. The difference was that one grew on purpose and the other grew by accident — which, in a field that reinvents itself every quarter, mostly means didn't grow at all.

That's the quiet, brutal arithmetic of security work: skills here decay fast. The thing you were expert in two years ago is half-relevant now. So "standing still" isn't a neutral position. It's a slow slide backward, measured against a landscape that won't stop moving and a peer group that doesn't either.

Growth is a decision, not a weather pattern

Most people treat their own development as something that happens to them — a course their boss books, a project that lands on their desk, a title that arrives on schedule. That's growth by accident, and it's how you end up the year-three tech doing year-one work. Deliberate growth is the opposite: you decide what you're becoming, then you arrange the small daily evidence that you're becoming it.

Ravi Das, writing for people building cybersecurity practices, is clear-eyed about certifications — the Security+, the CySA+, the CISSP. They're proof to a client, yes, but their real value is as a forcing function: a deadline that makes you actually learn the material. His warning is just as sharp — a wall of badges that never became real competence is collecting, not growing. Stephen Covey filed the same idea under his seventh habit, sharpen the saw: the woodcutter too busy sawing to sharpen the blade cuts slower every hour. Renewal isn't time away from the work; it's what keeps the work sharp.

"As long as you live, keep learning how to live." Seneca · Letters to Lucilius (Letter 76)

Seneca treated learning as a lifelong obligation, not a phase you finish. He also kept a practice worth stealing wholesale: each evening he reviewed the day — what he'd done badly, what he could do better, what he'd learned. Not to punish himself, but to run a deliberate improvement loop on an ordinary Tuesday. Johanna Rothman aims the same lens forward: periodically reassess your own value, retire the skills that no longer serve, deliberately add the ones the next year will need. And Alan Weiss adds the part people skip — you grow into the bigger role by deciding to back yourself before you feel ready, not by waiting until permission arrives.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Grow your own — it's cheaper than the alternative

The ambitious tech you didn't develop doesn't stagnate quietly forever. She leaves — for the shop that gave her the cert budget and the harder problems. Replacing her costs you months of recruiting, ramp, and lost client knowledge that no onboarding doc captures. A training line item and real hours to use it is not a perk. It's the cheaper side of a trade you're making either way.

  • Fund the cert and the time to earn it. Das is blunt that certifications are forcing functions — but only if people have room to study, not just a voucher and a wish. A budget without protected hours is a benefit nobody can spend. Pay for both, and the badge actually becomes competence.
  • Build a path the ambitious can climb without leaving. Rothman's reassess-your-value habit needs somewhere to point. If the only way up is out the door, your best people walk it. Name the rungs — L1 to L2 to lead, generalist to specialist — so growing in place is a real option, not a polite fiction.
  • Let people stretch one size above themselves. Juniors become seniors by being handed work slightly past their reach, with a net under them. The triage call you'd normally take, the client conversation you'd normally lead — hand it over sometimes. Discomfort with a safety line is exactly how the curve bends upward.
Living it

If you're on the team

Own your roadmap — nobody's coming to write it for you

The tech still doing year-one tickets in year three wasn't sabotaged. He waited — for the course to be offered, the project to be assigned, the title to arrive. Seneca's point lands hardest here: the responsibility to keep learning is yours, not your manager's. The good news is the same as the demand — it's in your hands, starting this week.

  • Pick the door, then work backward to the key. Decide what role you want in a year, find the one cert or skill that opens it, and reverse-engineer the steps. "Get better at security" is a wish. "Pass the SC-200 by November" is a plan with a forcing function attached.
  • Run Seneca's five-minute review. Once a week, ask: what did I actually learn, and what would I do better next time? Write it somewhere. It costs five minutes and it converts ordinary tickets — the ones that otherwise just blur past — into deliberate practice.
  • Teach what you learn — out loud. Reputation in a small shop is built by being the person who shares the thing they just figured out. Write the runbook, answer the junior's question properly, post the gotcha to the team channel. Teaching is how learning sets, and how the room starts asking for you by name.
  • Back yourself before you feel ready. Weiss's quiet truth: almost nobody feels ready for the next role — you grow into it by stepping up, not by waiting for a certainty that never comes. The colleague who got the break usually just raised their hand first. Stop under-rating yourself; the gap is smaller than your nerves claim.

The two techs, three years on

Stand them side by side now and the gap looks enormous — one trusted with the hard calls, the other stuck. But rewind it and there was never a single fork in the road, no big break that landed on one and missed the other.

There was just a hundred small Tuesdays. One of them booked the study hour and kept it. Ran the five-minute review. Raised her hand for the call she wasn't quite ready for, and was ready for the next one because of it. The other meant to, someday, when things calmed down — and things never calmed down, because they never do.

Growth is a decision you make on ordinary Tuesdays, when nothing's forcing you and no one's watching. It compounds so slowly you can't feel it happening — and then one day a client asks for you by name, and you realize the curve bent a long time ago, one quiet choice at a time.

Try this this quarter

On the team: pick ONE skill or cert to move toward this quarter, and book the first hour this week — on the calendar, with a real time. Leaders: ask each person what they want to be good at in a year, then carve out one protected hour for it.
