The Human Layer · The dichotomy of control

Own your circle

Why the calmest person in the incident is usually the one who knows exactly what is — and isn't — theirs to carry.

It's 4:52 on a Friday. A client's finance lead just forwarded a wire she says she "already paid." The sender's domain is off by one letter. Somewhere an invoice got intercepted, and forty-one thousand dollars is on the move. Your phone turns into a slot machine of notifications. Your stomach drops.

Here's the thing nobody tells you on day one of this job: that drop in your stomach is not the emergency. It's your reaction to the emergency — and it's the first thing in the whole mess you can actually govern.

You didn't cause the spoofed domain. You can't un-send the wire by force of will. But the next ninety seconds — your tone on the call, the order you work the steps, whether you reach for blame or for the runbook — those are entirely yours. That narrow band of yours is the whole subject of this article.

The oldest idea in incident response

Nearly two thousand years before anyone wrote a SOC playbook, a former slave named Epictetus opened his handbook with a line that still cuts straight through a bad Friday:

"Some things are within our power, and some things are not." Epictetus · Enchiridion

Within our power: our judgments, our choices, our effort, the next thing we do. Not within our power: other people, outcomes, the past, the threat landscape, the thing that already landed in the inbox. The Stoics built an entire philosophy on refusing to confuse the two.

Marcus Aurelius ran the Roman Empire while plague and war ran straight at him. The private notebook he kept — never meant for us to read — contains a line that could be taped to every monitor on the floor:

"If you are pained by any external thing, it is not this thing that disturbs you, but your own judgment about it — and it is in your power to wipe out that judgment now." Marcus Aurelius · Meditations, Book 8

This is not "don't care." The Stoics cared enormously. It's a line drawn down the middle of every stressful moment: on one side, the things that arrive whether you like them or not; on the other, the only territory you actually govern. Their hard-won observation was that nearly all of our suffering comes from spending ourselves on the wrong side of that line.

If this feels familiar, it's because the idea never went away. Stephen Covey built the most quoted habit in modern business writing on it — what he called your Circle of Influence versus your Circle of Concern. His advice was simple: pour your energy into the smaller circle you can actually move, and over time it grows. Viktor Frankl, writing from inside something far worse than a bad Friday, located human freedom in exactly the same spot — the space between what happens to us and how we choose to respond. Different centuries, same map.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Your team inherits your relationship with that line

A leader who treats every alert as a personal insult teaches the room that incidents are occasions for panic and finger-pointing. A leader who, in the worst hour, visibly narrows to "okay — what's ours to do in the next ten minutes?" teaches something far more valuable: that competence is a posture, not a mood. Three things sitting squarely inside your circle:

  • Turn down the noise that was never theirs. Alert fatigue, a client who treats 11 p.m. like noon, a vendor outage nobody can fix tonight — these bleed your team dry on the "not up to you" side. Tightening policy, tuning alerts, and setting real on-call boundaries is some of the highest-leverage work you control.
  • Model proactive language. The room copies the owner's verbs. "We can't, the client won't, Microsoft always" is contagious — and so is "here's what we can do." Covey was blunt about this: reactive language quietly hands your power away.
  • Go first on accountability. David Maister's whole body of work on professional firms lands on one unglamorous point — trust is built when the person at the top holds themselves to the standard before anyone else is asked to. You cannot delegate the example.
Living it

If you're on the team

Your circle is bigger than your job title

You might be three months into an L1 seat, certain none of this is yours to decide. That's the exact trap the Stoics warned about — mistaking "I'm not in charge" for "I have no power here." You have more than you think.

  • Find the gap, then act. When an alert lands and your pulse jumps, there's a beat before you respond. Epictetus and Frankl both pointed at that beat. Ninety seconds of "what do I actually know, and what's the first correct step?" beats ninety seconds of spiraling, every single time.
  • Watch your verbs. "I have to," "they're making me," "there's nothing I can do" are usually false and always corrosive. Trade them for "I'll handle the part that's mine." It changes how you feel and how the room sees you.
  • Spend your worry where it pays. The breach at a company you don't run, the policy you can't change today — note it, then put it down. Your attention is finite; aim it at the ticket in front of you, which is the only one that actually exists right now.
  • Own the miss cleanly. You'll get one wrong eventually. Your response stays in your circle even when the mistake wasn't fully yours. "Here's what happened, here's what I'm doing about it" is the most senior thing a junior can say.

Friday, 4:52, replayed

The wire is still moving. Nothing about that changed because you read an article. But picture two versions of you holding it.

One is already drafting the sharp message about why the client clicked, stomach in a knot, three steps behind the problem. The other has gone quiet, opened the recall-and-report runbook, phoned the bank's fraud line first because that's the step that can still matter at 4:53, and saved the human conversation for Monday — without blame, because blame was never on the side of the line that helps.

Same incident. Same forty-one thousand dollars at risk. Completely different person carrying it. That difference is the human layer — and unlike almost everything else on a Friday afternoon, it's entirely yours.

Try this on your next live incident

Before you touch anything, name two things out loud: one that is not up to you, and one that is. Then start with the second one.
