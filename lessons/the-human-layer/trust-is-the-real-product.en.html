The Human Layer · What clients actually buy

Trust is the real product

Your client will never read your Conditional Access policies. They wouldn't understand them if they did. What they buy, renewal after renewal, is the belief that you've got it handled.

It's 11 p.m. on a Tuesday. A client — the bakery-supply owner who still calls his laptop "the computer" — forwards you an email that scared him. "Got this. Looks like our bank. Real?" You glance at it from bed: lookalike domain, urgent tone, a link that resolves nowhere good. You thumb back one line: "Phishing. Delete it, don't click. You're fine — I'll confirm in the morning."

He exhales and goes to sleep. He believes you completely. Not because of anything in that message — he couldn't tell a spoofed header from a recipe — but because of the eleven months of small, kept promises behind it.

Here's the uncomfortable truth at the centre of this job: your client will never read your Conditional Access policies. They wouldn't understand them if they did. They can't audit your firewall rules, verify your patch cadence, or grade your incident response. What they actually buy, every renewal, is one thing — the belief that you've got it handled.

The product you can't put on an invoice

Most trades hand the customer something they can inspect. The plumber's leak stops or it doesn't. In security, almost everything you do is invisible to the buyer and unverifiable by them. The breach that didn't happen leaves no receipt. The policy you tuned at 2 a.m. looks identical, from the client's chair, to having done nothing at all.

So the thing they're really purchasing isn't the configuration. It's trust — and trust, unlike a firewall rule, can't be bought off a shelf or copied from a competitor. David Maister and his co-authors put a usefully blunt shape on it: trust is roughly (credibility + reliability + intimacy) divided by self-orientation. Stack up all the expertise you like in the numerator. The whole thing still collapses the instant the client senses you're serving yourself ahead of them.

"No longer talk at all about the kind of man that a good man ought to be, but be such." Marcus Aurelius · Meditations, Book 10 (George Long translation)

That line is the whole craft compressed into a sentence. Trustworthiness isn't a thing you assert in a sales deck or a value statement on a website. It's a thing you are — demonstrated, mostly, in the small steps done correctly when absolutely no one is checking. Which, in security, is always. Stephen Covey called the running tally of these moments an Emotional Bank Account: every kept promise is a deposit, every dropped one a withdrawal, and the balance is the only thing that's standing the night a client forwards you something scary at 11 p.m.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Compete on trust, not on the feature list

There is always a cheaper stack and a flashier dashboard down the road. You will lose the spec-sheet war eventually, and you can't win it forever. What no competitor can undercut is a client who, after years of being right about you, simply doesn't want to find out whether the next provider would have answered at 11 p.m. That's the moat. Build it on purpose.

  • Sell the four terms, not the tooling. Maister's equation is a literal checklist for how to be chosen: visible credibility, dead-reliable follow-through, real intimacy with the client's business, and a self-orientation low enough that they never wonder whose interests you're serving. Most MSPs pitch the numerator and quietly lose on the denominator.
  • Make reliability a system, not a heroic effort. "We always come through" is not a strategy if it depends on one person remembering. Tickets that don't fall through cracks, promises with dates attached, follow-ups that fire whether or not anyone's having a good week — engineer the consistency so it survives your worst Tuesday.
  • Be honest about what the client doesn't need. The fastest way to torch trust is to look self-interested — and the fastest way to build it is to say "you don't need that tier, save the money" when it costs you the upsell. Every time you put their interest ahead of the invoice in front of a client, the denominator drops and everything above the line gets bigger.
Living it

If you're on the team

You make the deposits — one small promise at a time

You might think the trust relationship lives above your pay grade, in the owner's handshake and the contract. It doesn't. It's built and spent at your seat, in fragments most people never notice — the callback you remembered, the answer you didn't fake, the step you did right when no one would ever check. Those are your deposits, and they're the whole account.

  • Close every loop you open. Reliability, up close, is almost never heroism — it's remembering to follow up. "I'll get back to you by 3" and then actually doing it at 2:50 is worth more to the relationship than the cleverest fix delivered a day late and unannounced.
  • Say "I don't know — I'll find out by 3." Credibility is honesty about your limits, not the appearance of having none. A confident wrong answer spends trust you'll never get back; an honest "let me check" deposits it. The client can forgive not knowing. They struggle to forgive being bluffed.
  • Do the unwatched step right. Marcus's whole point: be the good technician, don't perform one. Nobody is auditing whether you actually verified the backup restored or just ticked the box — and that is precisely the moment that decides whether you're trustworthy. They never check. Act as if the check is constant, because the consequences are.
  • Carry the relationship like it's yours. When a client feels you genuinely care how their business does — not just whether the ticket's closed — that's the intimacy term, and L1s build it as surely as owners do. It costs nothing and it's almost impossible to copy.

Tuesday, 11 p.m., replayed

The bakery-supply owner is asleep now, phone face-down, that scary email already in his trash. He never thought about why he believed your one line. He just did.

But that belief wasn't built tonight. It was built across eleven months of returned calls, honest "you don't need that"s, backups you verified when nobody was watching, and a dozen small "by 3" promises kept by 2:50. Tonight just spent a little of what all of that deposited. The reply was free because the account was full.

That's the strange economics of the only product you actually sell. Trust is slow to build and instant to spend — one self-interested move, one bluffed answer, one dropped promise can overdraw an account it took years to fill. But fill it, and you've got the one deliverable that renews itself: a client who sleeps because you're awake.

Try this this week

On the team: pick one promise you made this week and close the loop on it today, visibly — even if it's just "done, as promised." Leaders: find one place you're recommending something that serves you more than the client, and fix it before they ever notice.
