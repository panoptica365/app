The Human Layer · Burnout as an engineering problem

Beating burnout (the 16-month clock)

Burnout in security isn't a character flaw or a sign you weren't cut out for this. It's the predictable failure mode of an always-on job — and the good news about failure modes is that you can design against them.

By the fortieth alert of the day, you stop reading them. That's not laziness. That's a human being doing exactly what humans do when everything is urgent: nothing feels urgent anymore. The thirty-ninth was a false positive. So was the thirty-seventh. Your eyes slide over the fortieth on the way to closing the tab, and you almost — almost — miss that this one is real.

Add the 2 a.m. pager that turned out to be a storage threshold nobody had tuned in a year. Add the tech two desks over who hasn't had a real weekend since April, who answers "fine" a half-second too fast. None of these are dramatic. That's the point. Burnout doesn't arrive as a breakdown. It arrives as a slow flattening — of attention, of care, of the thing that made you good at this.

Here's the reframe this article is built on: a burned-out analyst is not a wellness problem. It's a security hole. Missed alerts, sloppy changes, the best people walking out the door eighteen months in. Once you see burnout as an operational risk, you can do what you already do well with every other risk — engineer against it.

An infinite queue, and what it does to people

Most jobs have a bottom to the inbox. Security doesn't. The queue refills while you sleep, the threat landscape never signs off, and there is always one more thing that could be checked. An infinite queue meets a finite human, and the human is the part that gives. The only question is whether it gives slowly, by design, or suddenly, by neglect.

Ravi Das knows the suddenly. A former CISO who wrote candidly about burning out of the chair, he reports a number that should stop any owner cold: the average tenure of a CISO before burnout runs around sixteen months. He doesn't frame this as a parade of weak people. He frames it as a design failure — a role built without renewal, boundaries, or relief, asked to absorb an infinite queue with a finite nervous system. Treat burnout as a flaw in the person and you'll keep replacing people. Treat it as a flaw in the system and you can fix the system.

The Stoics had a sharp diagnosis for one big driver of that flattening. We don't burn out only on the work that lands — we burn out on the rehearsal of work that never does. Seneca put it plainly:

"We suffer more often in imagination than in reality." Seneca · Letters to Lucilius (Gummere translation)

Half the weight of the 2 a.m. pager isn't the page. It's the hour you spend afterward staring at the ceiling, running the breach that didn't happen. The Stoics had a counter-move for this — not denial, but rehearsal done deliberately. Premeditatio malorum: calmly picturing what could go wrong, in advance, on your own terms. Done that way, the imagined disaster loses its ambush quality; when the real version arrives, it arrives smaller, because you've already met it once in daylight. Marcus Aurelius offered a companion move he called the view from above — zoom out far enough and the crisis shrinks to its true size, one incident among many, on one Tuesday, at one of your clients. Neither trick makes the work lighter. Both keep your imagination from doubling its weight.

Stephen Covey called the same maintenance "sharpen the saw" — his seventh habit. Picture someone sawing a log for hours, exhausted, making no progress, too busy to stop and sharpen the blade. Renewal is sharpening the blade: rest, movement, learning, the things that keep you cutting. And the cruel twist is that you need it most exactly when you feel you can least afford it — in the thick of the quarter, when the queue is deepest. Johanna Rothman, writing for independent consultants, pushes the same idea into the calendar: manage your own health and schedule as deliberately as any client engagement, and be willing to walk away from the engagement that can only be done by destroying yourself. The boundary isn't indulgence. It's inventory management for the one asset you can't reorder.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Burnout is a staffing and tooling decision, and it's yours

You can't legislate how your team feels, but you own almost everything that shapes it — the alert volume, the on-call rules, the example at the top of the org chart. Ravi Das would tell you the sixteen-month clock isn't fate; it's the sum of decisions nobody revisited. Here's where your leverage is highest:

  • Tune alerts so the fortieth still means something. Every false positive spends a little of your team's attention, and attention doesn't refill. False-positive load isn't a tuning nicety — it's a wellbeing line item. Cut the noisiest source and you've just bought back focus for the alert that's real.
  • Set on-call boundaries and then honor them. A rotation that exists on paper but never lets anyone fully off the hook is worse than none, because it pretends to protect people while quietly draining them. Real coverage means someone can put the phone face-down and trust it.
  • Watch the quiet high-performer. The person most likely to burn out is rarely the one complaining. It's the steady one running on fumes, covering for everyone, answering "fine" too fast. Rothman's advice cuts both ways: protect their health as deliberately as you'd protect a deadline.
  • Model rest, because the team copies the owner. If you answer email at midnight and never take a real day off, no policy will convince your people that rest is allowed. They watch what you do, not what your handbook says. Logging off is the message.
Living it

If you're on the team

The blade is yours to keep sharp

You may not set the alert thresholds or the on-call rota. You still own more of your own resilience than you think — and the habits below are the difference between giving slowly, by choice, and giving suddenly, by surprise.

  • Protect the renewal that keeps you sharp. Covey's saw is yours to sharpen. The walk, the workout, the evening that's genuinely off — these aren't what you do once the work is done. They're how you stay good enough to do it. Skipping them to grind harder is sawing with a duller blade.
  • Name the dread, then test it against reality. When the 2 a.m. worry spins up, ask Seneca's question: is this catastrophe happening, or am I rehearsing it? Most dread is rehearsal of things that never happen. Naming it out loud usually shrinks it to its real size.
  • Take the real weekend. Not the weekend where you "just check the dashboard." A weekend you actually leave is worth three you spend half-watching. The queue will be there Monday; it always is. You're no good to it flattened.
  • Tell urgent from important. If everything is a sprint, you'll sprint at the trivial and the critical alike, and burn out on both. Sort before you run. The fortieth alert deserves a glance, not a 100-metre dash.
  • Ask for help before the tank is empty, not after. "I'm at my limit" said on Tuesday is a planning conversation. Said after you've already missed something, it's an incident review. Earlier is always cheaper.

The 2 a.m. pager, replayed

It's 2 a.m. again. The pager goes off. But this time you're in a shop that engineered against the flattening.

The alert is one of the few that survived a tuning pass, so when it fires, it means something — you don't have to wonder. There's a real backup on call tonight, not a name on a spreadsheet, and the rotation actually let you be off last week, so the tank isn't empty. You glance, you triage, and it's not the kind of thing that can't wait. A message comes back from the owner, who saw the same page: "Go back to sleep — this can wait till 8." And it can.

The work didn't get smaller. The infinite queue is still infinite, the threat landscape still never sleeps, and the fortieth alert tomorrow will still be a slog. What changed is the system around the human — fewer false alarms, real relief, a leader who drew the line before the line got drawn by exhaustion. Burnout was never your weakness. It was a risk in the design, and the design is something you can change.
