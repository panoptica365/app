The Human Layer · Communication

Say the hard thing, clearly

Most security failures are communication failures wearing a technical costume. The risk was known. The words just didn't land.

The vulnerability was in the report. Page nine, in a sentence that began "additionally, legacy authentication remains enabled across the tenant…" It was true. It was accurate. It was, technically, disclosed. And nobody read page nine.

Three months later, that exact gap is how the attacker walked in — past the MFA nobody could enforce on a protocol nobody remembered was open. At the post-mortem, somebody pulled the old report and found the line. The room went quiet. The risk had been known the whole time. The words just never made it across.

This is the uncomfortable truth under a lot of security work: the failure usually isn't ignorance. It's transmission. The signal existed; it didn't arrive. And transmission — unlike the threat landscape — is almost entirely yours to fix.

Security runs on messages getting through

Strip a security program down to what actually keeps a client safe and you find a chain of small communications: the alert that gets understood instead of ignored, the warning that lands instead of scrolling past, the question a junior feels safe enough to ask before they click. Break any link and the tooling underneath stops mattering. You can have a flawless config and still lose, because the person who needed to know never really knew.

The old voices on persuasion said this a century ago, and nobody has improved on it. Carnegie's whole argument was that people don't move because you told them they were wrong — they move on what they want and feel. So you talk in terms of the other person's interests, and you never open by putting them on the defensive. Tell a client their setup is reckless and you've won an argument and lost the room. Show them what it costs them, and they lean in.

Covey put the same idea into a single habit: seek first to understand, then to be understood. His complaint was that most people don't listen to understand — they listen to reply, waiting for their turn to fire back the fix. And Maister, writing about how trusted advisors actually earn that word, reframed the whole thing: communication is a two-way conversation in plain language, not a broadcast. The less you try to sell or lecture, the more people open up. The page-nine sentence wasn't a conversation. It was a broadcast into an empty room.

Notice what changed between the panels: not the facts, only the form. The left side is complete and unread. The right side is a message built for a person who is busy, anxious, and not a security expert — which describes almost every client you'll ever serve.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Build a shop where bad news travels fast and upward

Every culture decides, in advance, how easily a hard message moves. In a healthy shop, the worst finding of the week reaches the right person within the hour. In a brittle one, it dies on page nine — or in a technician's inbox where it felt too awkward to escalate. That's not luck. It's something you set.

  • Make plain language a house rule with clients. Jargon feels like expertise; it's actually a moat that drowns your own value. Maister's point was that trust is built in plain conversation — so insist that every client-facing finding gets translated out of acronyms before it ships. If the bookkeeper can't act on it, it isn't done.
  • Make "I don't understand, say it again" a safe sentence. In every meeting, internal or client-facing, the person who admits confusion should be thanked, not quietly judged. Covey's whole habit collapses if people are too proud to say they didn't follow. You set that tone in the first thirty seconds, every time.
  • Reward the escalation, not just the fix. The technician who flags an ugly risk before it's polished into a clean report is doing exactly what you need. If bad news only travels when it's been sanitized, you've trained your team to slow down the one signal that should move fastest.
Living it

If you're on the team

Your job isn't to be right. It's to be understood.

You can produce a perfectly accurate report and still fail the client, because accuracy that nobody reads protects nobody. The craft you're building is making the true thing also the landed thing — and that's a skill, not a personality trait.

  • Write the email that gets read. Lead with the risk and the ask, not the chronology. Nobody needs the story of how you found it before they know what it is and what you want. Put the most important sentence first; everything else is appendix.
  • Translate tech into their stakes. Clients don't care about legacy authentication; they care about money, downtime, reputation, and the auditor who's coming in March. Carnegie's old rule still holds — talk in terms of their interests. "This could let someone wire your money" beats any protocol name you could cite.
  • Ask before you assume. Half the friction in a client conversation comes from answering a question they didn't ask. A quick "what's worrying you most about this?" tells you which version of the truth they actually need to hear.
  • When you disagree, don't open with "you're wrong." That first sentence decides whether the next ten are heard. Carnegie's move was to lead with a question instead of a verdict — and Covey's was to listen for the client's real fear, not for your cue to fire back the fix. The fear is usually the thing worth answering.

Page nine, replayed

Picture the same report, written by someone who learned this. The finding hasn't changed — legacy authentication is still enabled, still dangerous, still buried in the technical appendix where the detail belongs. But it no longer lives there.

Now the first thing the client sees, before the charts and the methodology, is three sentences in words the bookkeeper understands: there's an old login door still open; it lets people skip the security you're paying for; closing it takes us twenty minutes on Thursday and here's the one approval we need from you. The bookkeeper reads it. The owner reads it. Thursday, the door closes.

The vulnerability didn't change. The sentence did. That's the human layer — and it's the difference between a finding that protects someone and a finding that just sat there, technically disclosed, waiting on page nine.

Try this

Take your last status update to a client. Find its most important point — then rewrite it as the first sentence, in words your client's bookkeeper would understand. If you can't say it that plainly, you don't understand it yet.
