The Human Layer · Purpose, and the invisible scoreboard

Why this work matters

Most of your best work is invisible — measured in disasters that never arrive. This is about reconnecting the ticket queue to the people on the other end of it, so the dull parts still feel like they're for something.

You will probably never get a thank-you card for the breach that didn't happen. That's the strange grief of this job: your best work is invisible, measured in disasters that never arrive. The bakery whose backups you quietly fixed last spring. The dental office whose old admin account you killed before anyone phished it. The twelve-person nonprofit that would have simply folded after a ransomware hit — and instead kept making payroll, never knowing how close it came.

None of them sent flowers. None of them know your name. From the inside, the work can feel like a faceless ticket queue — close one, another opens, repeat until Friday. It's easy to forget there's a person at the other end of every ticket, because the system is built to show you the ticket and hide the person.

This article is about reconnecting the two. Not with a slogan, but by remembering — on the dull days especially — exactly whose lights you're keeping on. Because purpose isn't a poster on the wall. It's the thing that makes a 2 a.m. callout bearable.

The scoreboard nobody can see

Marcus Aurelius, reminding himself why to bother on a hard morning, wrote a line that lands squarely on this work:

"Men exist for the sake of one another. Teach them then or bear with them." Marcus Aurelius · Meditations, Book 8 (George Long translation)

He's not being sentimental. He's stating the job description for being a person: we exist for the sake of one another. Read it next to a ticket queue and the dull parts change color. Patching a server isn't tending a machine — it's keeping faith with the twelve people whose payroll runs on it. Reviewing the fortieth alert isn't admin — it's standing watch for someone who will never know your face. The framing dignifies the work without inflating it. You're not a hero. You're someone who exists, in part, for the sake of the people on the other end of the ticket.

The trouble is that the framing decays under load. Tickets are concrete and immediate; the people they protect are abstract and somewhere else. So we have to put the people back in the picture on purpose. Stephen Covey called this habit "begin with the end in mind" — define the why before you act on the what, and let a clear purpose steer the thousand small decisions underneath it. He pushed people to write a personal mission statement for exactly this reason: a one-line answer to "what is this all for?" that you can hold up against a bad day.

David Maister, who spent a career studying professional firms, made the same point at the level of the whole shop. A firm needs a real, believable purpose — not a hollow mission statement laminated in the lobby that no one acts on. When the purpose is genuine, he argued, it does practical work: it simplifies a thousand decisions, because you can ask "does this serve who we're here to protect?" and the answer is usually obvious. And it quietly attracts the right people — the ones who want the work to mean something stay, and the ones who don't, leave. Purpose isn't decoration. It's a sorting mechanism and a decision shortcut, both at once.

Leading it

If you run the shop

Make the purpose concrete, true, and hard to ignore

Your team can't draw motivation from a scoreboard they never see. Part of your job is to keep dragging the underwater part of the iceberg into view — not as a pep talk, but as a fact about the work. Maister's test is the bar: is the purpose believable, or is it poster-ware?

  • Define a purpose you'd actually defend. "Who do we protect, and from what?" should have a real answer — small businesses that can't survive a breach, say — not a laminated phrase about excellence. Maister's whole point is that a believable purpose does work; a hollow one just collects dust in the lobby.
  • Bring client outcomes back to the bench. When a tech's quiet fix stops something, say so, by name: "the thing you patched in March is why that office wasn't in the news last week." The person behind the ticket only becomes real if someone keeps pointing at them.
  • Hire and fire by the mission. Covey would tell you to begin with the end in mind here too. The people who want the work to mean something will stay if the meaning is real; protect that, and let the purpose do the sorting Maister described.
  • Tell the close calls, not just the wins. The breach you stopped, the business still running because of an unglamorous Tuesday — these are the real scoreboard. If you don't narrate them, no one will, and the queue will look faceless to the people working it.
Living it

If you're on the team

Keep one real face in front of the queue

You don't need the org to hand you a mission to find one. The meaning is already in the work — your job is to keep it in view on the days the tickets blur together into noise.

  • Write your own one-line "why." Covey's personal mission statement, shrunk to a sentence: why this work, for you? Make it true, not noble-sounding, then test it against your worst day. A "why" that survives a bad Tuesday is the one to keep.
  • Remember the human on the other end. Marcus's reminder is the whole move — we exist for one another. The ticket isn't an abstraction; it's someone's payroll, someone's patient records, someone's small shop. Picture them once before you close it.
  • Connect the dull task to the open door. Patching, reviewing alerts, rotating a key — none of it feels heroic. But the bakery stays open because of the boring parts, not in spite of them. The unglamorous task is the protection; they're the same thing.
  • When it feels pointless, zoom in. Not out — in. Pick one real person your work protects and hold them in mind for a second. Abstraction drains meaning; a single concrete face restores it faster than any motivational quote.

The bakery, on a Friday

Picture the bakery owner you'll never meet. It's Friday afternoon. The staff get paid, the lights are on, the ovens are warm, and tomorrow there'll be bread. The owner has no idea that eight months ago a ransomware crew was one weak password away from ending all of it — and that someone they never spoke to closed that door on a routine Tuesday and moved on to the next ticket.

No card came. No headline ran. There's no notification anywhere that says "disaster averted." The owner made payroll on Friday and never knew how close it came — and that, exactly that, is the point. The arrow that stopped short of the shield doesn't show up on any dashboard. It only shows up in a small company that's still here.

That's the scoreboard for this job: mostly underwater, rarely thanked, measured in the disasters that never arrived. It's a strange way to keep score — but it's a real one, and it's worth keeping. On the 2 a.m. days, it's the only one that helps.
