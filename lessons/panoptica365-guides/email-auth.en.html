Email authentication

The Email Auth tab: each domain's SPF, DKIM and DMARC — read from public DNS, graded, and watched for the drift that re-opens a domain to spoofing.

A tenant's other surfaces live inside Microsoft 365. Email authentication lives in public DNS — the SPF, DKIM and DMARC records that tell the world's mail servers who is allowed to send as your customer's domains. The Email Auth tab reads those records for every domain, grades the posture, and watches the managed ones for the kind of quiet regression that re-opens a domain to spoofing.

What you see

Pick a tenant and click Refresh; Panoptica reads each domain's live DNS and lays out a card per domain:

  • A weighted posture score with a letter Grade (A–F) — the headline number, weighted by how much each record matters.
  • A row of per-record cards — MX, SPF, DKIM, DMARC, plus the supporting records DNSSEC, MTA-STS and TLS-RPT — each marked Pass, Partial, Fail or Absent, with a plain-language finding (for example, "SPF ends in ~all (soft fail) — move to -all once the sender list is confirmed").
  • Detected senders — the mail providers Panoptica inferred from the records (Microsoft 365, a marketing platform, a ticketing system), so a missing DKIM selector points at a specific provider to fix.
  • An AI analysis that reads the whole posture and tells you what to fix first.

A domain that shouldn't send mail at all is handled differently: it's scored as a non-mail domain (anti-spoof posture) — the right answer there is a hard SPF -all and a DMARC p=reject that say "nothing sends from here," not a full sending setup. Microsoft's own routing domain (onmicrosoft.com) is shown for reference but not graded.

The three that carry the weight

The supporting records harden transport, but spoofing protection rests on three:

  • SPF lists the servers allowed to send for the domain. The tail matters: -all hard-fails forgeries; ~all only soft-fails them; +all authorizes anyone — effectively no protection. SPF also has a hard limit of 10 DNS lookups; overflow makes strict receivers ignore it entirely.
  • DKIM signs outbound mail so receivers can verify it wasn't tampered with. A published, signing selector is a pass; a revoked (empty key) or test-mode selector means mail is effectively unsigned.
  • DMARC ties the two together and tells receivers what to do with mail that fails: p=reject (enforced), p=quarantine (to junk), or p=none (monitor only — no enforcement). Dropping to p=none is the single most common way a domain quietly becomes spoofable again.

Refresh, and drift

Refresh reads live DNS, re-scores, and regenerates the analysis on demand. For managed tenants, Panoptica also re-checks the monitored domains daily and diffs them against the last good snapshot. A regression — DMARC downgraded, a DKIM selector revoked or vanished, SPF loosened, a transport record removed — raises an email-auth drift alert that names exactly what changed, before → after. Improvements are recorded silently; only changes that make spoofing easier alert.

That matters because this drift leaves no sign-in log and no audit trail — it happens at the DNS host, not in Microsoft 365. A registrar migration, or a "helpful" web host that republishes its own SPF, can undo months of anti-spoofing work, and nothing inside the tenant would show it. This tab is the early warning.

Accepting a change

When the change was intentional — you (or the domain owner) switched a provider or tightened a record — open the domain and click Accept on the flagged change to set the current records as the new baseline; the linked alert resolves. When it wasn't, fix it at the DNS host (restore the DMARC policy, re-publish the DKIM selector, tighten SPF) and Refresh to confirm it cleared.

Read-only by design

Panoptica reads public DNS — it never changes your records. Every fix is made by you at the registrar or DNS host. The tab tells you what's wrong and watches for regressions; the edit is always yours.
