Deploy configuration bundles

Group your tenants, bundle your CA and Intune settings, and push a whole baseline to many tenants in one preflight-guarded job.

The Deployments section (sidebar → Deployments) is where you stop configuring tenants one at a time. You organise your tenants into groups, assemble a Configuration Bundle of Conditional Access policies and Intune settings, then push the whole baseline to a tenant — or a whole group of tenants — in a single job. The result is identical to deploying each setting by hand from the tenant dashboard, minus the deploy-deploy-deploy clicking.

Start in Settings

Before any of this works, go to Settings and set up two lists: Service Tiers and Sales Reps. These are what let you tag each tenant and build groups by tier or by rep. Managing these lists is an admin-only job — do it once, up front. Skip it and your only grouping option is picking tenants by hand.

Step 1 — Set up your lists, then tag your tenants

On the Settings page you'll find two small managers: Service Tiers (e.g. Bronze, Silver, Gold) and Sales Reps (the people who own accounts). Add the entries that match how your MSP actually works. Then open each tenant's edit modal and assign its Service Tier and Sales Rep from the dropdowns — both are optional, but they're the raw material for dynamic groups and for filtering.

When someone leaves

You can't hard-delete a tier or rep that's still assigned to tenants (or referenced by a group rule) — Panoptica blocks it and lists exactly which tenants and groups are in the way, so a departing rep can't silently empty a group. Deactivate the entry instead: it disappears from the assign dropdown for new work while existing tenants keep their assignment until you reassign them.

Step 2 — Build your tenant groups

Open Deployments → the Groups & Bundles tab. The left pane lists your tenant groups, each with a count badge showing how many tenants it holds. Click Add Group and choose one of two kinds:

  • Manual — a checklist of every tenant; tick the ones you want. Best for arbitrary buckets ("pilot tenants", "the three I'm migrating this month").
  • Dynamic — a rule over service tier and/or sales rep, combined with all (AND) or any (OR). For example, "Silver tier and rep = John". Membership is live: as you onboard a tenant that matches, it joins the group automatically — no maintenance.
A tenant belongs to many groups

Groups overlap on purpose — the same tenant can be "Gold tier", "Marie's accounts" and "Quebec" at once. That's fine: when you later deploy to two groups that share a tenant, it's deployed to once, never twice.

Bonus: once your groups exist, a Filter by Tenant Group dropdown appears on the Heatmap and the global Trends dashboards. A sales rep can now see how just their book of business is doing — and with a dynamic group, that view stays current on its own.

Step 3 — Assemble a Configuration Bundle

The right pane is your Configuration Bundles. A bundle is a named recipe — a list of the CA policies and Intune settings you want a tenant to have. Click into a bundle to edit it, then add items from your existing libraries:

  • CA policies — pick templates from your CA Policies library. For each one, choose the State it should land in: Report-only (the default and the safe choice — the policy is created but not enforced) or On (enforced) (live and blocking). This is set per policy, right in the bundle, so you decide once and never fiddle with it at deploy time.
  • Intune settings — pick templates from your Intune Policies library. For each one, choose an assignment target: no assignment, all users, or all devices. There's no default — you pick deliberately.

You can also override the alert routing per item if this bundle should notify differently from the template's default. Nothing deploys while you're building — a bundle is just the recipe. Build it, review it, reuse it across the whole fleet.

"Group" always means a group of tenants

Bundle targeting is deliberately coarse — all users or all devices in a tenant, never a security group inside a tenant. When Panoptica365 says "group", it always means a group of tenants. Per-user or per-device exceptions are made afterwards, in the individual tenant.

Step 4 — Deploy (and the preflight that guards it)

Click Deploy… at the top of the section. A short modal walks you through it:

  1. Choose the target kind — a single tenant, or a tenant group.
  2. Multi-select from the list that appears (you can pick several tenants, or several groups).
  3. Pick the bundle to deploy.
  4. Click Submit.

Submit does not deploy anything. It builds the job and runs a preflight — a dry set of read-only checks — then drops you on the Job Queue tab to review the results. Nothing is written to any tenant until you explicitly click Deploy on the armed job.

What the preflight checks

For every setting on every tenant, the preflight flags anything worth your attention before a single write:

CheckWhat it does
ConsentThe tenant must have a valid admin-consent token, or its items are blocked.
Managed onlyAudit-only or disabled tenants are blocked — a dynamic group can't accidentally sweep a monitor-only tenant into a live write.
Already presentIf the tenant already has that policy, the item defaults to skip. You can switch it to overwrite.
Named locationFor CA policies, checks that the tenant's named locations are readable (they're auto-created at deploy).
Target changeIf an Intune setting already exists with a different assignment target, the note warns you rather than changing it silently.

Use the bulk control — "Action to take if a policy or setting already exists" — to set every already-present item to Skip or Overwrite at once, or set individual items by hand for exceptions. When you're satisfied, the Deploy button arms (a confirmation names the bundle and the number of tenants first, because it means it).

Preflight can go stale — the worker re-checks

Consent can lapse between the preflight and your click. The engine re-checks consent and managed status at execution time too, so a tenant that changed in the meantime fails its own item gracefully instead of erroring the whole job.

The Job Queue

The Job Queue tab is your window into every deployment, live and historical. Each job is collapsible in three levels — Job ▸ Tenant ▸ Setting — with a rollup at each level ("6/8 tenants OK", "2 items failed"). It refreshes on its own while a job runs.

Under the hood the engine is deliberately careful: one job at a time, a small number of tenants in parallel, and strictly one setting at a time within a tenant (CA before Intune), each waiting for a clean success before the next. Microsoft throttling is handled automatically with backoff.

Partial success is the normal outcome

Across a group of tenants with different consent and licensing states, some items will fail — and that's fine. A failure on tenant 4 never stops tenants 5 through 8. Every item records its own success or its own error message, so the queue reads like "deployed to 6, tenant 4 failed on 2 CA policies — here's why." Finished jobs stay in the queue for 90 days as a deployment history.

One thing the preflight does not pre-judge is licensing. An Intune setting that needs a SKU a tenant doesn't have will fail its own item at execution, with Microsoft's capability message in the error — the rest of the job still lands.

What happens in the tenant afterward

A bundle deploy is identical to a hand deploy

Everything a bundle pushes is created exactly as if you'd deployed it yourself from the tenant dashboard. The CA policies and Intune settings appear on that tenant's own CA Policies and Intune Policies tabs, drift detection guards them from that moment, and exemptions apply — no difference at all. The bundle is just the fast way in.

Because of that, per-tenant exceptions belong in the tenant, not in the bundle. If one customer needs a policy tweaked or an exclusion added, make the change in their tenant dashboard afterward, then accept the drift there. The bundle got you to a consistent starting line across the fleet in one shot; the tenant tabs are where you handle the one-offs.

Who can do what

Operators can build groups, assemble bundles, and run deployments — this is day-to-day fleet work, not an admin-only lever. Viewers can look but not fire. Managing the Service Tier and Sales Rep lists lives on the admin-only Settings page. Every action — group and bundle changes, and every deployment with its full resolved target list — is written to the audit log with your name on it.

Conditional Access can lock a tenant out — that part is on you

Report-only is the default for a reason: a CA policy deployed enforced across a group, with a mistake in it, can lock admins out of every one of those tenants at once. Panoptica365 gives you the seatbelt — report-only first, a named-tenant confirmation, a full audit trail — but it will not save a policy you didn't design and test. Prove your CA templates on one tenant in report-only before you set a bundle item to On (enforced) and aim it at a group.

Operating note

The bundle is a force multiplier in both directions. Deployed carefully, it makes a consistent security baseline across an entire book of business a five-minute job. Deployed carelessly, it multiplies a single mistake by the size of the group. Treat the first enforced-CA deploy to any group with the same respect you'd give a change window on your biggest customer — because that's effectively what it is.
